Intelligent tuning of quantum dot devices

Semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) offer a promising path toward scalable quantum computing, with compatibility across a range of materials and fabrication methods. Yet, operating QD-based qubits reliably requires precise, continual adjustment of multiple gate voltages to control individual electrons. These systems are highly sensitive to environmental fluctuations and fabrication-induced disorder, making them dynamic and challenging to manage manually, particularly at scale.

In Q-ACE Lab, we are developing a modular, autonomous, and platform-agnostic framework for QD device characterization and calibration, designed to overcome these challenges. In our approach, we consider the tuning process as a series of multiple phases, starting with device characterization and ending with optimizing readout, and with each completed stage informing the execution of the consecutive one. The individual phases, including device bootstrapping, state tuning, virtual gate construction, and charge state tuning, are implemented with domain-specific algorithms and machine learning tools.

By leveraging physical insight, pattern recognition, physics-informed strategies, and realtime feedback, our systems automate tuning with high success rates across experimental platforms. In particular, our system has demonstrated reproducibility and scalability across GaAa, Si/SiGe, and Ge/SiGe architectures, both on data from samples fabricated in an academic cleanroom as well as on an industrial 300-mm process line, contributing toward full-stack automation of quantum processors. More broadly, our work illustrates the power of combining machine learning with experimental quantum systems and marks a step toward robust, scalable control of quantum hardware.

Network analysis

We employ social network analysis to understand factors that affect student persistence in introductory physics courses. Our work resulted in a number of surprising findings. For instance, we found that social integration within a peer community is more important than grades in predicting persistence for certain cohorts of students. This work has been highlighted in Science, Nature Physics, and as the Editors' Suggestion in Physical Review.
Likert-style surveys are widely used research instruments to assess respondents' preferences, beliefs, or experiences. We proposed a complex network analysis approach to identify and study the interconnectedness of items in Likert-style surveys, providing an analysis tool that complements the traditionally employed methods such as PCA or EFA.
