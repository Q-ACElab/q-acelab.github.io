Current team members

Justyna P. Zwolak, PI

Justyna is a mathematician at the National Institute of Standards and Technology and an Affiliate Fellow in the Joint Center for Quantum Information and Computer Science (QuICS) at the University of Maryland. Her research centers on developing machine learning algorithms to optimize and automate quantum computing experiments, focusing on semiconductor quantum dots and cold atom systems. For her pioneering work at the intersection of artificial intelligence and quantum technologies, she received the Excellence in Research in Applied Mathematics award from the Washington Academy of Sciences, the US Department of Commerce Bronze Medal Award, and the 2025 Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers.

Donovan Buterakos, Postdoctoral Associate @UMD [2023 – present]

Donovan applies machine learning to advance quantum information technologies, with a focus on automating the tuning, virtualization, and operation of quantum dot devices. He developed and released an open-source Python library that implements a robust physics simulation, enabling the seamless generation of diverse, high-quality training datasets. These datasets, which include feature labels and closely mimic experimental data, are used to train machine learning models for identifying and classifying features in quantum dot measurements. Donovan integrates these models into software stacks that support automated device operation in experiments worldwide.

Merritt Losert, NRC Postdoctoral Associate @NIST [2025 – present]

Merritt is a physicist studying quantum computing in semiconductor qubits. He wants to build a scalable quantum computer, using the tools of physics and machine learning. In graduate school, he studied valley physics and atomic disorder in Si/SiGe quantum dots, focusing on how disorder affects scalability and how to mitigate the impacts of disorder through device design. As an NRC Postdoctoral Associate in the Q-ACE Lab at NIST, he is developing automated tools for device analysis and tune-up, using the methods of machine learning and image analysis.

Zach Merino, Postdoctoral Associate @UMD [2025 – present]

Zach studies mathematics and numerical computation in the context of physics and engineering. He earned a B.Sc. in Physics with a minor in Mathematics from the University of North Carolina at Charlotte, where he also completed an M.Sc. in Applied Physics with a focus on computational statistics. He later obtained a Ph.D. in Physics with a concentration in quantum information from the University of Waterloo. His current research interests center on the autonomous control of qubits in physical systems, with an emphasis on scalable, fault-tolerant quantum computing. His work explores physics-informed algorithmic, machine learning, and artificial intelligence approaches.

Anantha Rao, Graduate Student @UMD [2025 – present]

Anantha’s research lies at the intersection of quantum information and condensed matter physics, with a focus on many-body quantum systems to both understand their behavior and enable scalable quantum devices. His current work spans the theory and experimental automation of semiconductor quantum dot devices, where he develops methods for quantum characterization, verification, and validation (QCVV) and explores the use of machine learning for quantum systems. He also employs numerical approaches such as tensor networks to address complex many-body problems, with the broader goal of advancing robust and scalable quantum technologies.

Daniel Schug, Graduate Student @UMD [2023 – present]

Daniel’s research lies at the intersection of interpretable machine learning and scientific data. He develops tools and methods that enable scientists to employ and interact with machine learning and mathematical models in experimental settings, with a primary focus on the tuning, calibration, and optimization of semiconductor-based quantum dot devices. He is also building a comprehensive software framework for algorithmic control and auto-tuning of these devices, alongside a suite of software solutions and prototypes that support scientific and machine learning workflows. His work emphasizes human-driven interaction with models – spanning training, inference, and explanation – to ensure both usability and interpretability in practice.

Research opportunities in Q-ACE Lab

Machine Learning (ML) and artificial intelligence are beginning to broadly impact physics: from probing the evolution of galaxies to calculating quantum wave functions to discovering new states of matter. This postdoctoral research opportunity centers on developing autonomous ML-driven systems for measurement, calibration, and control of quantum systems and quantum computing platforms.

Working closely with scientists in the Physics Measurement Laboratory at NIST as well as with external collaborators, a successful candidate will extend our exisitng ML-driven autonomous systems for state assessment, calibration, and control of quantum technologies. This work will specifically focus on combining ML algorithms with classical data analysis and control techniques to develop robust in situ (i.e., in real-time, during the operating experiment) assessment of quantum systems. The candidate will develop custom calibrtion and optimization algorithms that combine ML with domain knowledge to establish fully automated control protocol. The proposed protocols will be implemented and validated experimentally. Current applications of interest include, but are not limited to, semiconductor quantum dots and cold atom systems. Candidates with an interest in experiments with ultracold atoms should contact Ian Spielman .

Key words: machine learning; artificial intelligence; autonomous control; optimization; quantum dots; cold atoms;

Eligibility: Open to U.S. citizens. Applicants must have held the doctorate for less than five years as of the application deadline.

Additional informtion: NIST participates in the February and August reviews. See NRC Research Associateship Programs for additional infomration about the application process.

Q-ACE Lab is seeking motivated Ph.D. students to join our efforts in automating the characterization, tuning, and control of semiconductor quantum dot devices. Our work bridges physics, computer science, and engineering to enable scalable quantum computing technologies.

Research focus: You will contribute to developing algorithms and workflows for real-time device control, with an emphasis on integrating machine learning methods into experimental pipelines. Our group works with:

  • Supervised learning for state recognition,
  • Unsupervised learning for pattern discovery and clustering,
  • Reinforcement learning for automated physical and quantum gate tuning and adaptive calibration, and
  • Physics-informed learning approaches for robust extrapolation and explainability.

We collaborate closely with experimental teams at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, TU Delft, University of Copenhagen, UCLA, and Intel Corp., giving students the opportunity to work with both high-fidelity simulations and real experimental devices.

Qualifications

  • Strong interest in quantum devices, experimental physics, and machine learning.
  • Proficiency in Python and familiarity with ML frameworks (e.g., scikit-learn, PyTorch, or TensorFlow).
  • Background in condensed matter physics, quantum information, or related areas is desirable but not strictly required.

Why join us? You will be part of a dynamic, interdisciplinary team advancing the frontier of quantum device automation. To apply, send a CV, statement of interest, and contact information for two references to Dr. Justyna Zwolak.

Q-ACE alumni

Harry S. Chalfin, M.S. in Physics [2023 – 2025]

Harry was a graduate student in the Q-ACE Lab from 2023 to 2025. His research focused on machine learning-driven optimization of spin readout in quantum dot devices. In particular, he explored the use of reinforcement learning methods to redesign readout circuits and improve fidelity for silicon spin qubits. His work earned him the 2024 IMPACT Award from the American Physical Society’s Group on Data Science, which recognizes graduate students who “have made noteworthy progress in their academic careers in the Data Science field.” Harry graduated from the University of Maryland in May 2025 with an M.S. in Physics.

Danielle Middlebrooks, NIST Director’s Postdoctoral Fellow [2022 – 2023]

Danielle was the NIST Director’s Postdoctoral Fellow in Q-ACE Lab at NIST from 2022 to 2023. Her reseach centered on developing fully automated methods for characterization, calibration, and control of semiconducting quantum dot devices, focusing on the first phase of the tuning process that brings the quantum dot device into an operational mode, the so-called “bootstrapping” or “cold-start tuning.”

Lisa Ritter, NRC Postdoctoral Fellow [2022 – 2023]

Lisa was an NRC Postdoctoral Fellow in Q-ACE Lab at NIST from 2022 to 2023. Her research focused on integrating machine learning techniques with physics-based heuristics and modeling to analyze data from cold atom experiments. She contributed to developing methods for the automated detection of topological excitations – such as kink solitons and solitonic vortices – in experimental images of Bose-Einstein condensates.

Joshua Ziegler, NRC Postdoctoral Fellow [2020 – 2022]

Josh was an NRC Postdoctoral Fellow in Q-ACE Lab at NIST from 2020 to 2025. His research focused on developing machine-learning-driven aproaches for charge tuning in quantum dot devcies. He also worked on enhancing QFlow – the in-hous build quantum dot simulation – by incorporating synthetic noise typical of quantum dot experiments. Josh left NIST in 2022 to join Intel Corporation as a Quantum Computing Research Scientist.
